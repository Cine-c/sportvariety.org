Jayson Tatum Erupts for 38 Points as Celtics Advance to Eastern Conference Semifinals

By Marcus Johnson March 12, 2026 8 min read

The TD Garden was electric on Wednesday night, 19,156 fans rising to their feet as Jayson Tatum drained a step-back three-pointer with 3:42 remaining in the fourth quarter, pushing the Boston Celtics' lead to 17 points and effectively sealing a 112-98 victory over the Miami Heat in Game 5 of their first-round playoff series. With the win, the Celtics advance to the Eastern Conference Semifinals with a 4-1 series victory, keeping their title defense firmly alive.

Tatum was nothing short of spectacular, finishing with 38 points on 13-of-22 shooting, including 6-of-9 from beyond the arc. He added 8 rebounds, 5 assists, and 2 steals in what was arguably his most complete playoff performance since the Celtics' championship run in 2024. It was the kind of night that reminded everyone in the building — and everyone watching at home — why Tatum is considered one of the five best players on the planet.

A Series Defined by Boston's Depth

The Celtics entered the 2026 playoffs as the second seed in the Eastern Conference, carrying the weight of expectation that comes with being defending champions. Miami, seeded seventh, was widely viewed as a dangerous first-round opponent. The Heat had pushed Boston to seven games in the 2023 Eastern Conference Finals and had a playoff pedigree under Erik Spoelstra that demanded respect. And for two games, Miami lived up to that billing.

After splitting the first two games in Boston — the Celtics taking Game 1 convincingly 108-91 before the Heat stunned the Garden crowd with a 104-101 overtime victory in Game 2 — there was a genuine sense that this series could become a prolonged battle. Miami's zone defense was causing problems for Boston's half-court offense, and Jimmy Butler was playing with the kind of intensity that has defined his playoff career.

But Games 3, 4, and 5 told a different story entirely. The Celtics won all three by double digits, outscoring Miami by a combined 48 points. Head coach Joe Mazzulla made critical adjustments to break the Heat's zone, deploying more high pick-and-roll actions with Tatum and Kristaps Porzingis that forced Miami's defense to make difficult rotation decisions. The results were devastating for the Heat.

In Game 3, it was Jaylen Brown who led the way with 31 points and 9 assists. Game 4 belonged to Porzingis, who dominated the paint with 28 points and 12 rebounds. And in Game 5, Tatum took the mantle, delivering the kind of closeout performance that championship teams require from their best player. That depth — the ability to have a different star step up each night — is what separates the truly elite teams from the merely good ones. Tatum's Fourth-Quarter Takeover

The game was competitive through three quarters. Miami, refusing to go quietly, had cut a 14-point halftime deficit to just 6 entering the fourth, with Bam Adebayo imposing himself in the paint and Tyler Herro knocking down a pair of contested threes. The Heat faithful in the crowd — and there were more of them than Boston fans would care to admit — were making themselves heard. The momentum was shifting.

And then Jayson Tatum decided the series was over.

In a breathtaking four-minute stretch early in the fourth quarter, Tatum scored 14 consecutive points for the Celtics. It began with a driving layup through contact, converting the and-one free throw with ice in his veins. Then came back-to-back three-pointers — one from the left wing off a Brown hockey assist, another from the top of the key after a Porzingis screen that left Tatum wide open for just a fraction of a second. He didn't need more than that.

The sequence was capped by a mid-range pull-up jumper, the kind of shot that Tatum has refined over the past two seasons to become virtually unguardable. When the ball dropped through the net, the lead was back to 17, and the Heat called timeout. But the damage was done. Miami managed to trim the lead to 14 in the final minutes, but the outcome was never seriously in doubt.

"I've been in these moments enough times now to know what's required. When I felt the game tightening up, I just told myself to be aggressive, trust my work, and let the game come to me. This team has been through everything together. We know how to win when it matters."
— Jayson Tatum, post-game press conference

Jaylen Brown: The Perfect Running Mate

While Tatum commanded the headlines, Jaylen Brown's contribution to the series cannot be overstated. The co-Finals MVP from 2024 averaged 24.6 points across the five games, shooting an efficient 48% from the field. In Game 5, he added 22 points, 6 rebounds, and 7 assists, providing the steady complementary scoring that allowed Tatum to operate without the burden of carrying the entire offensive load.

Brown's defensive intensity was equally crucial. Tasked with guarding Jimmy Butler for much of the series, he held the Heat star to 21.4 points per game on 39% shooting — well below Butler's season averages. In Game 5 specifically, Butler managed just 16 points on 6-of-18 shooting, his frustration visible as he picked up his fourth foul midway through the third quarter and had to sit for an extended stretch.

The Tatum-Brown partnership has matured into one of the most formidable duos in the NBA. Their combined ability to score, defend, and facilitate makes the Celtics extraordinarily difficult to game-plan against. When one is rolling, the other can play off the ball. When both are firing, as they were for stretches of this series, the Celtics are nearly unbeatable. Both players continue to build their legacies among the top athletes in professional basketball.

Miami's Season Ends with Pride

For the Miami Heat, the loss brings an end to a season that exceeded many expectations. Picked to finish outside the play-in tournament by most preseason projections, Miami's run to the seventh seed and a competitive first-round series against the defending champions was a testament to Erik Spoelstra's coaching and the team's relentless competitive spirit.

Bam Adebayo was Miami's best player in the series, averaging 23.2 points and 11.4 rebounds. His physicality in the paint gave Porzingis problems at times, and his passing from the high post created open looks that the Heat didn't always convert. Tyler Herro added 19.8 points per game but struggled with consistency, shooting just 29% from three in the series after hitting 39% during the regular season.

Butler, who turns 37 this September, faces an uncertain offseason. His contract situation remains unresolved, and while his leadership and toughness are undeniable, the declining efficiency numbers raise questions about whether Miami can build a contender around him for one more run. Spoelstra, as always, will figure out a way to make it work — but the path back to the Finals looks steep from South Beach.

What Awaits in the Eastern Conference Semifinals

The Celtics will now face the winner of the Cleveland Cavaliers-Orlando Magic series, which is tied 2-2 heading into Game 5. Cleveland, the third seed, would present a more conventional challenge with their size and half-court execution. Orlando, the sixth seed, would offer a different test with their length and defensive versatility. Either way, Boston will be heavy favorites.

But Mazzulla knows better than to look ahead. The Celtics were eliminated in the second round in 2023 after winning the championship the year before, a painful reminder that playoff success is never guaranteed, no matter how talented the roster. The coaching staff has already begun preparation for both potential opponents, with film sessions scheduled for Thursday and Friday while the team enjoys a brief rest.

Porzingis's health will be monitored closely during the break. The 7-foot-3 Latvian appeared to tweak his right calf late in Game 5, though he insisted it was nothing serious in his post-game comments. Given his injury history, the Celtics' medical team will take no chances. A healthy Porzingis is essential to their championship aspirations — his ability to stretch the floor and protect the rim provides a dimension that no other team in the East can replicate.

A Title Defense Gathering Momentum

Four games into their playoff run, the Celtics look every bit like a team capable of repeating as champions. Their offense, which ranked second in the league during the regular season, has been even more efficient in the postseason, averaging 109.6 points per game on 48.2% shooting. Their defense, anchored by Brown, Derrick White, and Porzingis, has held opponents to 96.8 points per game in the series.

Tatum's 38-point closeout performance was the exclamation point on a dominant series, but it was also a declaration of intent. The 28-year-old is playing with a maturity and purpose that suggests he understands the rarity of this opportunity. Championship windows don't stay open forever, and Tatum is determined to make the most of this one.

As the confetti swirled at the TD Garden and the Celtics celebrated their advancement, Tatum stood at center court, looking up at the championship banner hanging in the rafters. He pointed to it, then pointed to the ground. The message was clear: that banner isn't enough. He wants another one. And on the evidence of this series, few would bet against him getting it.
